What is an Exam Hack?

In my career, I’ve taken (and passed, thank you very much) a lot of information technology

certification exams. In all, I've completed over twenty-five tests and approximately one

million multiple choice questions. Well, it seemed like a million.

After completing a few of these tests, I started to notice some trends. Regardless of the exam

I was taking, there were similarities in the study process, the test preparation, and the actual

testing experience.

Most CompTIA study materials discuss the technical exam details regarding technologies,

use cases, procedures, and the other various exam objectives. However, some of the most

important parts of the exams aren’t covered in any of these training courses.

This book is an effort to give you all of the information that you won't find in those technical

guides. Make sure you study the official exam objectives, but also include these life hacks to

help you prepare, study, and pass your CompTIA certification exam.

I’ve spent my career in IT, and I’m proof that information technology can provide you with

job security, financial success, and a great life for you and your family. Good studies!

Hack #1: Get the Exam Objectives

If you’ve ever taken a certification exam before, then you know that most certification

providers will provide an outline that includes a high-level overview of what the exam will

cover. For the CompTIA exams, these exam objectives are very different than what you may

have found from other companies. CompTIA provides an extensive list of every possible

topic that might be covered on the exam, and they make them available for FREE.

You can follow this link to download the latest CompTIA Exam Objectives:

CompTIA Exam Objectives

https://www.professormesser.com/objectives/

These exam objectives should be the first thing you download for your studies, and they

make a perfect checklist to help guide your learning process. The Exam Objectives for

a single CompTIA exam are about twenty pages long and include topics summarized by

domain, a list of important acronyms, and a recommendation of useful hardware and

software for your lab. As you finish up your studies, you can use these objectives as a

checklist to see which topics may need a bit more work.

Although CompTIA mentions that these Exam Objectives may not include all topics covered

- n the actual exam, my experience has found that the exam questions stay very close to this
list. If you know everything listed in the Exam Objectives, you’ll do just fine on your exam.

Hack #3: Do Some Recon

Not all testing centers are alike, and some testing centers might actually work against you

during the exam. On one of my certification exam attempts, I entered a small office on the

second story of a run-down building to find that the testing “room” was literally a three-foot

square closet. Inside the closet was a small desk with a monitor, keyboard, and mouse, and

they had removed the closet door so my chair could be more easily pushed up to the edge of

the desk. I managed to pass that certification exam, but taking the test in a literal closet put

me at a significant disadvantage from the very beginning.

If possible, you should always visit your testing center prior to booking the exam. If the exam

center is in your area, it’s worth your time to stop by and see the overall facilities and the

testing room for yourself. Is the center easy to locate? Is parking available? How private are

the testing stations? All of these questions and your overall impression of the testing center

will be important on the day of the exam.

Hack #4: BYOM (Bring Your Own Marker)

In years past, you were given paper and a pencil when entering the exam room. This

provided the exam taker with a convenient way to take notes, perform calculations, and

write anything else that might come to mind in the testing room. The testing companies

found that having paper made it too easy for information to leave the testing center, so they

now provide a single page of laminated paper and the pages are cleaned after each exam.

To use these laminated pages, the testing centers also provide a dry erase marker.

Unfortunately, the markers used by many of these centers have large chiseled tips or have

been worn into a flat, wide tip. These markers work great if you’re building a poster for a

football game, but they’re useless for any type of note taking during the exam.

I’ve had great success by using my own fine-tip dry erase markers on the exam. You have to

clear the markers with the exam proctor, but most of them can appreciate the use of a proper

writing utensil for the exam. This is something that has to be approved by the exam proctor,

so it’s worth a call before the exam to verify that you can use your own fine-tipped dry erase

marker.

Important! Make sure you use a DRY ERASE marker. You don't want to use a permanent

marker on their laminated writing page.

If they say they already have dry erase markers that are perfectly suited for the exam, bring

yours anyway. Trust me on this one.

Hack #5: Schedule Your Exam NOW

It's human nature to wait until the very last minute to complete important tasks. A single

CompTIA exam, however, can take months of dedicated study before someone might

be ready to take the test. To help increase the urgency of this process, some folks like to

purchase a voucher and schedule their exam for a future date. With this date “locked in” and

written on the calendar, there’s often more motivation to get through the material before the

exam date arrives.

In practical terms, the exam date can always be rescheduled more than 24-hours before the

scheduled date and time. However, just knowing that a financial investment has already

been made and a slot has been selected can be enough of a catalyst to power through the

exam studies and prepare for exam day. This exam hack is more of a personal preference, but

it can sometimes make the difference when some additional motivation is needed.

Hack #6: Take Care of You

On exam day, you need to be the best version of you. On the evening before, you should

eat well and schedule yourself a quiet night in to make sure you get plenty of sleep. Before

your exam, make sure you have eaten and that you have nothing else on your schedule

immediately before or after your exam time. Your goal is to get the highest score possible

- n the exam, and sometimes external forces can create a conflict with that goal. If you plan
ahead, you’ll be calm, cool, and collected when exam time arrives.

Hack #7: Be the Early Bird

On the day of the exam, make sure you’re aware of the current weather situation. Both

summer and winter storms can create travel problems, so check for traffic conditions in

your area. If you've taken advantage of hack #3 and performed reconnaissance of the testing

center, then you won’t have any problem finding it on test day.

The exam center test schedules can vary widely. In busy geographies, every seat in the

testing center may be allocated for the entire business day. In this tight scheduling situation,

you don’t want to miss your testing window and it makes sense to arrive as much as thirty

minutes before your scheduled test time.

Even if your testing center is relatively slow and plenty of seats are available, planning to

arrive early provides your psyche with some significant advantages. You might have the

choice to start right away, or you may want to take the time to do some last minute studying

in the waiting area.

Self-Serving tip: The Professor Messer Course Notes PDF or book makes a perfect last

minute study guide!

Hack #8: Don't Lose Time

You want to be at your best during the exam, so make a restroom stop before checking in for

your exam. This might appear to be an obvious hack, but exam day can bring some anxiety

and sometimes even the most important things can be missed. Not taking advantage of

this hack is something that I often hear as a common mistake from test takers, so it’s worth

mentioning here.

The problem becomes very clear once the exam has started. Leaving the room during the

exam requires interaction from the proctor, and your exam clock doesn’t stop while you’re

away from the desk. Remembering to take a bathroom break may seem trivial, but not

following this hack can literally cost you the exam.

Hack #9: Don't be Thrown

Of all the hacks in this book, this one is probably the most difficult to follow. Once the

exam begins, you have ninety minutes to complete all of the questions, and the exam will

automatically end when the clock ticks down to zero. A timer will always be available at the

top of the screen.

The first handful of questions on the exam are performance-based questions. These

questions are very unlike multiple choice questions. They could be fill-in-the-blank,

matching, command-line labs, drag-and-drop, or sorting. The performance-based questions

have thrown test takers who weren’t expecting such detailed and time-consuming questions

right at the beginning of the exam, so don’t let the clock eat away at your available time!

Hack #10: Flag your Questions

This is one of my favorite hacks, and this simple technique has provided me with many exam

points over the years. During the exam, you have the option to mark a question by clicking

a check box at the top of the screen. When you look at the exam review screen, all of the

marked questions will have the letter "M" next to them. You can then quickly select the

marked questions for further review.

This allows for a few different exam strategies. My personal approach is to go through the

questions as quickly as possible, and any questions that I’m not 100% certain of the answer

will get marked. As you might expect, this will leave me with a very large list of marked

questions after my first time through. However, I don’t have to double check any questions

that weren’t marked, and I can quickly start narrowing down my review questions from

there.

You might want to flag the really tough questions, or use the mark as a way to quickly return

to the questions that you think you can answer correctly after some additional review. Either

way, the option to flag the questions can be a useful strategy, and you should use it to your

advantage during your exam.

Hack #11: Use the Exam to Tell You the Answers

Wouldn’t it be great if the exam would tell you the answers to the questions? For this hack,

that’s exactly what happens.

Many CompTIA exams present about 90 questions across all of the exam domains. That’s a

lot of questions, and some of those questions will sometimes reveal the answer to a different

question, or a set of answers will jog your memory about an important fact related to a

different question.

This can be especially helpful with the performance-based questions. My recommended

strategy is to completely skip the performance-based questions at the beginning of the

exam, and instead start the exam with the multiple-choice questions. Once I’m through

the multiple-choice questions, I circle back to the performance-based questions and use

the information from the multiple-choice questions to hopefully provide me with some

additional hints or tips. With these additional details, it can often make it easier to get

through the more extensive performance-based tasks.

This can also work the other direction, where the details in the performance-based

questions can be used to help answer multiple-choice questions. Every exam is a random set

- f questions, so you won’t know exactly what you’ll be faced with until the exam starts.

Hack #12: Fix Problems Immediately

Every exam center must conform to a standard set of requirements, but that still leaves a

lot of different seating arrangements, testing room layouts, and construction soundproofing

standards. Although it would be perfect if every exam center was as quiet as a library, this is

rarely the case.

If you’re in the middle of an exam and there are problems with noise or environmental

issues, make sure the exam proctor is aware of these issues immediately. Do not wait until

the exam is over to discuss any issues. Once your exam grade has been determined, it's too

late to solve any problems. The exam center knows that a comfortable work environment is

important, and they’ll do everything they can to keep things quiet and comfortable.

Some exam centers will provide earplugs, especially if the exam rooms tend to be on the

noisy side. It might also be a good idea to bring you own earplugs, as long as you make sure

the exam proctor clears their use before going into the exam room.

Hack #13: Answer Everything

We don’t know a lot about the grading process that CompTIA uses. We believe that each

question is worth a different number of points, but there’s no way to know what those point

values might be. The most pragmatic approach is to focus on doing well and hope that

enough exam points go your way.

Based on some non-scientific analysis, we believe that incorrect answers on a CompTIA

exam do not count against you. If you get a question wrong, they aren’t going to take points

away from your score. Obviously, incorrect answers also won’t help your final results.

This means that correct answers provide you with exam points, and questions that you get

correct with a guess will help your final score. If you find yourself short of time, you should

quickly pick an answer for every multiple-choice question. If you get the question wrong it

won’t count against you, and you’ll get points if you guess right!

Hack #14: Check your Results

When your exam is complete, you’ll be asked to answer some demographic questions, and

then your final score will be revealed. If you’ve done everything right, then you’ll see the

congratulations screen. If you didn’t make the cut, then you’ll see that as well. Bummer.

Your results will be available on the Pearson VUE site immediately after your exam. If you

were successful, then this online summary will be the validation of your passing score. If

you didn’t pass the exam, then this information documents the areas where you answered

incorrectly.

These results contain valuable information for your studies! As you plan to retake your

exam, you can use these results to help focus your studies. The areas marked as incorrect

should be the first place you start when studying for the next exam.
